# Lesson 2: Algebra Language

## Chunk 1: Variables and Constants

In algebra, we use letters to represent numbers that can change.  
These letters are called variables: x, y, a, b, g, etc.  
A constant is a number that never changes: 3, 10, 100, -5, etc.

## Chunk 2: Algebra Operations (Symbols and Meaning)

In algebra, we don’t just use words — we use symbols to represent operations.  
There are 4 main operations:

🔹 Addition  
Symbol: a + b  
Meaning: combine values  
Word form: “sum of a and b”  
Example:  
3 + 5 = 8  

🔹 Subtraction  
Symbol: a − b  
Meaning: take away or difference  
Word form: “difference of a and b”  
Example:  
9 − 2 = 7  

🔹 Multiplication  
Symbols:1: a·b,2: ab,3: (a)(b)  
Meaning: repeated addition / scaling  
Word form: “product of a and b”  
Example:  
4 × 3 = 12  
Important: we avoid “×” in algebra because it can confuse variables.

🔹 Division  
Symbols: 1: a ÷ b,2: a/b  
Meaning: splitting into equal parts  
Word form: “quotient of a and b”  
Example:  
10 ÷ 2 = 5  

🔹 Key idea  
Words like “sum”, “difference”, “product”, “quotient” tell you the operation.

## Chunk 3: Expressions vs Equations

In algebra, we have two important forms:

🔹 Expression: An expression is a math “phrase”.  
It can have:  
numbers  
variables  
operations  
But NO equals sign.  
Examples:  
3 + 5  
n − 1  
6x  
It is incomplete on its own (like a phrase in English).

🔹 Equation: An equation is a math “sentence”.  
It always has:  
an equals sign (=)  
Examples:  
x + 3 = 10  
2y = 8  
It shows two things are equal.

🔹 Key idea  
Expression = phrase (no “=”)  
Equation = sentence (has “=”)

## Chunk 4: Writing Algebra from Words

Algebra is often about turning English words into symbols.  
Certain words always signal specific operations.

🔹 Addition keywords  
“sum of a and b”  
“a plus b”  
“a increased by b”  
“b more than a”  
Example:  
“5 more than x” → x + 5  

🔹 Subtraction keywords  
“difference of a and b”  
“a minus b”  
“b less than a”  
Important rule:  
“less than” reverses order!  
Example:  
“6 less than x” → x − 6  

🔹 Multiplication keywords  
“product of a and b”  
“twice a” → 2a  
“3 times x” → 3x  

🔹 Division keywords  
“a divided by b”  
“quotient of a and b”  
Example:  
“x divided by 4” → x/4  

🔹 Key idea  
English words → algebra symbols → correct structure matters

## Chunk 5: Inequality Symbols (Comparing Numbers)

In algebra, we don’t always say two things are equal. Sometimes we compare them.  
That’s where inequalities come in.

🔹 Main symbols  
< means “less than”  
> means “greater than”  
≤ means “less than or equal to”  
≥ means “greater than or equal to”  
≠ means “not equal to”  

## Chunk 6: Grouping Symbols in Algebra

In algebra, we use grouping symbols to show which parts of an expression belong together.  
They work like punctuation in English (like commas and brackets).

🔹 Main grouping symbols  
( ) parentheses  
[ ] brackets  
{ } braces  

🔹 Simple example  
(2 + 3) × 4 = 20  
First do what’s inside the parentheses: 2 + 3 = 5, then multiply: 5 × 4 = 20.  

[2 + (3 + 1)] × 2 = 12  
Start with the inner parentheses: 3 + 1 = 4, then the bracket: 2 + 4 = 6, then multiply: 6 × 2 = 12.  

{2 + [3 × (1 + 1)]}  
Work inside step by step:  
(1 + 1) = 2 → then 3 × 2 = 6 → then 2 + 6 = 8.

## Chunk 7: Exponents (Powers)

Exponents are a shortcut for repeated multiplication.

🔹 Meaning  
An expression like:  
2³ means: 2 × 2 × 2  

🔹 Parts of an exponent  
Base → the number being multiplied (2)  
Exponent → how many times it is used (3)  

🔹 Simple examples  
3² = 3 × 3 = 9  
5³ = 5 × 5 × 5 = 125  

🔹 Key idea  
Exponents make repeated multiplication faster and shorter.

## Chunk 8: Order of Operations (PEMDAS Idea)

When an expression has many operations, we must follow a fixed order,  
so everyone gets the same answer.

🔹 Order rule  
Parentheses (grouping symbols first)  
Exponents  
Multiplication and Division (left to right)  
Addition and Subtraction (left to right)
